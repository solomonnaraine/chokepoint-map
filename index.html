Economic Methodology

Nowcasting Supply Chains

ChokePoint Map leverages IMF PortWatch vessel telemetry to nowcast maritime energy flows in near real time. Distinct tanker counts at port nodes function as a leading macroeconomic indicator: throughput density precedes official trade statistics by weeks, offering early signals on crude import demand, refinery utilization, and regional inventory builds.

When aggregate tanker traffic concentrates at primary energy hubs, arbitrageurs interpret the signal as tightening physical availability—often repricing Brent-linked futures before customs data confirms the shift. Conversely, dispersion toward secondary feeder ports may indicate rerouting, stock draws, or sanctions-driven trade lane substitution.

Throughput Tiers & Congestion Risk

Ports are classified by observed tanker vessel counts into three operational tiers. Hubs exceeding 150 tankers are modeled as global chokepoints where congestion, labor action, or geopolitical friction can transmit instantly into spot freight markets. Regional nodes (50–150) anchor localized energy security, while feeder ports (<50) carry limited systemic weight but remain relevant for granular trade analysis.

Haversine Vulnerability & Geographic Bottlenecks

Strategic vulnerability is computed by measuring great-circle distance from each port to five monitored chokepoints—Suez, Panama, Hormuz, Bab el-Mandeb, and Malacca—using Leaflet’s geodesic distance engine (equivalent to the Haversine formula on the WGS-84 spheroid).

Proximity to a bottleneck elevates a port’s supply chain vulnerability score because maritime trade is path-dependent: closing a narrow strait does not merely delay cargo—it forces costly rerouting. A Suez or Hormuz disruption typically redirects tankers around the Cape of Good Hope, adding an estimated 10–14 days to voyage times, increasing fuel burn, tightening effective vessel supply, and spiking time-charter equivalent (TCE) rates.

Geopolitical Shock Simulation

The shock simulator models counterfactual closure of individual chokepoints. Dependent ports—those whose nearest strategic gateway is closed—are escalated to CRITICAL (DISRUPTED) status. This reflects the economic theory of exposure through network dependency: a port need not sit inside a closed strait to suffer cascading cost shocks if its optimal routing topology requires transit through it.

Inflation & Freight Rate Transmission

Country-level inflation risk rises when import-dependent economies rely on ports proximate to disrupted corridors. Higher spot freight rates raise the landed cost of crude and refined products, feeding through to consumer energy prices, transport costs, and—where pass-through is rapid—headline CPI. ChokePoint Map surfaces these linkages as narrative inference, not forecasts; users should treat outputs as structured scenario analysis grounded in observed vessel geography.
